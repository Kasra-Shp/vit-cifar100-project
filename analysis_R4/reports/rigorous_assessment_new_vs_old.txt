EXECUTIVE SUMMARY -- BLUNT VERSION
==============================================================================

CONVERGED?  NO, NOT FULLY. Going from 3->6 epochs/step roughly halved the
under-training problem (100% -> 60% of (method,step) pairs still improving
>=3% on train CE at the final epoch; mean final-epoch relative improvement
dropped from 34.4% to 10.7%), but it did NOT fix it. The 4 methods WITHOUT
knowledge distillation (SimpleAvg, RankExt, SimpleAvg+FactorOrth,
RankExt+FactorOrth) are still clearly under-trained at epoch 6 (10-23%
relative train-CE improvement in the last epoch, every step). The 4 KD
methods are close to converged (mostly 0-3% in steps 2-5, but +21-23% in
step 1 for every method/every step-1, which is structural -- see below).
Extrapolating the current decay rate, the under-trained methods need
roughly 2 (median) to 4 (75th pct) more epochs on top of 6, with one
(method,step) outlier needing ~9 more. A flat EPOCHS=8-9 for all methods,
or better, a per-family budget (KD methods: 6 is enough; non-KD methods:
9-10), is the honest next step -- not "ship it."

BETTER THAN THE OLD RUN? MOSTLY YES, on accuracy: 7/8 methods improved
(mean +3.0pp all_seen accuracy), and the method ranking is IDENTICAL
between runs (Spearman rho = 1.000, 0/8 rank changes, top-2 unchanged and
in the same order: RankExt+FactorOrth+KD #1, SimpleAvg+FactorOrth+KD #2).
BUT the #1 method (RankExt+FactorOrth+KD, the headline result) got
marginally WORSE (68.15% -> 67.98%, -0.17pp) and the run is now showing
real overfitting behavior it did not show before: the epoch-over-epoch
"train down / val up" event rate quadrupled (5.0% -> 22.0% of transitions).
Net verdict: the NEW run is a better dataset to report from, but it bought
its accuracy gains partly by training longer into an overfitting regime
that the OLD run never entered, and the epoch/dropout/best-epoch-selection
changes were made SIMULTANEOUSLY, so "why exactly it got better" cannot be
fully isolated from these logs alone (see below).

READY FOR THE THESIS? NOT AS-IS. Concretely missing/needed before you can
defend these numbers cleanly:
  1. A run with EPOCHS ~8-9 (or a KD=6 / non-KD=9 split budget) so the
     under-trained methods actually converge, OR an explicit acknowledgment
     in the writeup that SimpleAvg/RankExt/+FactorOrth (non-KD) numbers are
     a lower bound, not a fair comparison point against the KD methods.
  2. Verification (from full logs / code, not the light export) of whether
     "best-epoch selection" is actually wired into which checkpoint gets
     merged/evaluated -- the diagnostic evidence in this export suggests it
     is NOT (final_train_ce/final_validation_ce in
     validation_diagnostics_by_method.csv match the literal last epoch
     exactly, in every case checked, even though the true per-step best
     epoch is earlier in 24/40 (60%) of (method,step) pairs). If true, the
     "mitigation" that was supposed to make longer training safe was not
     actually protecting accuracy computation, which is consistent with the
     rise in overfitting signatures.
  3. At least 2-3 more seeds. Both runs use seed=42 and only seed=42 --
     every number in this report (deltas, rank stability, "convergence")
     is a single-seed observation with no error bars. A -0.17pp regression
     on the #1 method and a +0.87pp gain on the #7 method are both well
     within plausible seed noise; they cannot currently be distinguished
     from noise.
  4. Real per-CL-step (1-5) accuracy trajectories. Both runs only export 3
     aggregated eval groups (first_step / later_steps / all_seen); the NEW
     run added `per_step_accuracy`, `backward_transfer`, `forward_transfer`
     columns to its schema but left them entirely empty (0/8 populated) --
     this is a genuine forgetting-curve gap, not just a "light export"
     omission, since the column now exists but is unused.

  RankExt                    20.96          23.56          +2.60        #8         #8

WHY DID RankExt+FactorOrth+KD (the best method) get WORSE?
This is the only method to regress, and it is the run's headline result,
so it deserves scrutiny rather than a shrug. Candidate explanations, and
what the data can/cannot tell us:
  - Overfitting from more epochs: PLAUSIBLE and best-supported. This
    method's own per-step diagnostics (Section 3) show its step-1 val CE
    is minimized at LOCAL epoch 4 (val CE 0.1380) and gets worse by epoch
    6 (val CE 0.1457, +0.0077) -- i.e. within step 1, 2 of the 6 epochs
    are actively counter-productive for validation loss on this method.
    Since accuracy appears to be computed from the literal final epoch
    (see Section 3's best-epoch-selection finding), this directly costs
    accuracy on the class group learned in step 1, which is exactly the
    group (first_step accuracy) most sensitive to a step-1-specific
    checkpoint choice.
  - Higher dropout (0.05->0.1) hurting a method that was already doing
    fairly well: PLAUSIBLE but not separable from the epoch-count effect
    with only these two runs -- both changed at once (see Section 6).
  - Random noise (single seed): CANNOT BE RULED OUT. -0.17pp on a 68%
    baseline is small; it is well within the range that could flip sign
    under a different seed. Given only one seed per run exists, this
    explanation cannot be distinguished from the two above using this
    data.
  HONEST ATTRIBUTION: the regression is real in this run, its direction is
  consistent with the method's own overfitting diagnostics (val CE rising
  in the exact CL step -- step 1 -- most responsible for this method's
  accuracy), but the magnitude is small enough, and the confound
  (epochs+dropout+best-epoch-selection all changed together, on 1 seed) is
  large enough, that "more epochs made this method modestly worse, likely
  via step-1 overfitting" is the best-supported claim -- not a proven one.

3. OVERFITTING AUDIT (stricter than the OLD-run pass)
==============================================================================

Overfitting signature = an epoch where train CE decreased from the
previous epoch (still learning the train split) while val CE increased
(generalization got worse), same definition as OLD's analysis_R3 pass.

  OLD (8-method subset, 3 epochs/step, dropout 0.05): 4/80 transitions
    (5.0%) show the overfitting signature.
  NEW (8 methods, 6 epochs/step, dropout 0.1):        44/200 transitions
    (22.0%) show the overfitting signature.

The overfitting-signature RATE more than quadrupled. The extra dropout
(0.05->0.1) was clearly not enough to fully offset doubling the epoch
budget. Per-method breakdown (25 transitions each: 5 steps x 5
epoch-to-epoch transitions):

  method                      overfit events   rate    max single-epoch
                                                        val-CE regression
  SimpleAvg                   10/25            40.0%   0.0281
  RankExt                      8/25            32.0%   0.0137
  RankExt+KD                   8/25            32.0%   0.0340
  SimpleAvg+KD                 7/25            28.0%   0.0177
  RankExt+FactorOrth           4/25            16.0%   0.0137
  RankExt+FactorOrth+KD        3/25            12.0%   0.0137
  SimpleAvg+FactorOrth         2/25             8.0%   0.0019
  SimpleAvg+FactorOrth+KD      2/25             8.0%   0.0173

Severity: all "max single-epoch val-CE regressions" are small in absolute
terms (<0.035 CE), so this is not catastrophic blow-up -- it is frequent,
mild overfitting, consistent across most methods, not a severe failure in
one or two of them. The train/val gap also grows steadily within most
steps rather than spiking (see analysis_R4/plots/3_train_val_gap_new_run.png).
Interestingly, FactorOrth methods (with or without KD) show the LOWEST
overfitting rates (8-16%) despite also being among the methods still
flagged "under-trained" in Section 1 -- the orthogonality regularizer
appears to have some genuine regularizing effect on validation stability,
independent of whether training CE has plateaued.

BEST-EPOCH SELECTION -- DID IT TRIGGER?
Per-step LOCAL best-epoch (argmin val CE within that step's 6 epochs,
computed directly from the loss history) differs from the final (6th)
epoch in 24/40 (60%) of (method,step) pairs -- i.e. if a best-epoch
checkpoint-selection mechanism were active, it WOULD have picked a
different, earlier epoch more often than not. Examples:
  RankExt+FactorOrth+KD, step 1: best=epoch 4 (val CE 0.1380), final=epoch
    6 (val CE 0.1457) -- selecting epoch 4 would save 0.0077 val CE.
  RankExt+KD, step 4: best=epoch 3 (val CE 0.7646), final=epoch 6 (val CE
    0.7814) -- selecting epoch 3 would save 0.0168 val CE, the largest gap
    found among the 40 (method,step) pairs.

HOWEVER: cross-checking validation_diagnostics_by_method.csv's
`final_train_ce` / `final_validation_ce` columns against the raw loss
history shows they are EXACT MATCHES to the literal last (step 5, epoch 6)
values for every method checked (e.g. SimpleAvg: both report train_ce=
0.015294, val_ce=0.094579; RankExt+KD: both report train_ce=0.519292,
val_ce=0.656874). This is evidence -- not conclusive proof, since the
light export does not show what checkpoint was actually merged forward
between CL steps -- that the reported "final" metrics (and, by extension,
the accuracy numbers in Section 2, which are computed once after all 5
steps complete) reflect the literal final-epoch weights of each step, NOT
a best-epoch-selected checkpoint. If so, the "best-epoch selection"
mitigation described as a planned code change (see
R3/.../reports/hyperparameter_consistency_notes.txt and the OLD run's
analysis_R3/reports/overfitting_analysis_R3.txt) exists as a MONITORING
diagnostic in this run's tables but does not appear to have been wired
into which weights actually get evaluated. This would directly explain
why the overfitting-signature rate quadrupled instead of being suppressed
by the mitigation that was supposed to catch it.

Separately: validation_diagnostics_by_method.csv's own "best_validation_ce"
/ "global_epoch_of_best_validation_ce" columns are computed as a GLOBAL
minimum across all 30 concatenated (step, epoch) pairs per method, not a
per-step local minimum. Because CL step 1 has fewer classes and
structurally lower val CE than later steps, this global minimum is biased
toward step 1 for every method with material step-1 improvement, and
should not be read as "the model overall was best at global epoch X" --
use the per-step local table (Section above) for anything about
early-stopping/checkpoint choice.

4. RANKING STABILITY
==============================================================================

Comparing NEW's 8-method ranking against OLD's ranking restricted to the
same 8 methods (i.e. excluding OLD's 2 delta-trace-only methods, which are
absent from NEW and were removed by supervisor request, not because of any
result):

  Spearman rank correlation: rho = 1.000 (p < 0.0001)
  Rank changes: 0/8 methods changed rank position
  Top-2 stable: YES -- RankExt+FactorOrth+KD is #1 and SimpleAvg+FactorOrth+KD
    is #2 in BOTH runs, matching the user-supplied reference values
    (68.15% / 61.19% old vs 67.98% / 63.80% new).

This is a genuinely strong result: with 8 methods spanning a >40pp accuracy
range, getting the exact same total order across two runs with materially
different epoch/dropout/selection settings is a real signal that the
RELATIVE comparison between methods is robust, even though the ABSOLUTE
numbers moved (Section 2) and the training itself is not fully converged
(Section 1).

CAVEAT -- SINGLE SEED: both runs use seed=42 and ONLY seed=42. A perfect
rank correlation across two runs of the SAME seed with different
hyperparameters is reassuring but is not the same claim as "this ranking
is robust to random initialization/data order." It is entirely possible
that this particular seed happens to produce a stable ordering while other
seeds would not; with n=1 seed per configuration there is no way to
estimate the variance of the ranking itself, only observe that it did not
move between these two specific runs. Do not present rho=1.0 as "the
ranking is proven stable" in the thesis without this caveat, and ideally
without at least 2-3 more seeds to actually estimate rank variance.

6. HONEST WEAKNESSES (what a thesis committee would flag)
==============================================================================

  1. SINGLE SEED, SAME SEED. Both runs use seed=42 exclusively. This means
     (a) no error bars/confidence intervals exist for any number in this
     report, (b) the ranking-stability result (Section 4) and the
     regression on the #1 method (Section 2) cannot be distinguished from
     seed noise, and (c) using the identical seed across the two runs
     means any genuine seed-driven difference did NOT get a chance to
     appear, which could make the runs look MORE similar/stable than they
     would be across independent seeds -- rho=1.0 could partly be an
     artifact of sharing a seed, not evidence of true robustness.

  2. SIMULTANEOUS CONFIGURATION CHANGES. Between OLD and NEW, at least
     THREE things changed at once: EPOCHS (3->6), LORA_DROPOUT (0.05->0.1),
     and (nominally) best-epoch checkpoint selection. Any accuracy or
     convergence delta in this report is a delta across all three changes
     combined -- it is NOT possible, from these two runs alone, to say
     "epochs did X, dropout did Y, best-epoch-selection did Z." Isolating
     these would need at least 2 more runs (e.g. 6 epochs/dropout 0.05,
     and 6 epochs/dropout 0.1/no best-epoch-selection) as an ablation.

  3. BEST-EPOCH SELECTION APPEARS NOT TO BE ACTUALLY APPLIED (Section 3).
     If accuracy truly is computed from the literal final epoch rather
     than a best-val-CE epoch, then the run's documented mitigation for
     "more epochs -> more overfitting risk" was not actually protecting
     the numbers reported, and the observed quadrupling of the overfitting
     -signature rate (5.0% -> 22.0%) is the predictable consequence. This
     should be verified directly against the training code (not just this
     light CSV export) before the next run.

  4. TWO METHODS DROPPED, NOT RE-RUN. The delta-trace methods
     (simple_avg_delta_orth, rank_extension_orth_delta_trace_lam_50) that
     existed in OLD are absent from NEW by supervisor request, not because
     of a result -- fine for the current 8-method comparison, but means
     NEW cannot be used to check whether delta-trace's OLD ranking
     (#4 and #10) would have held up under 6 epochs/dropout 0.1.

  5. STEP-1 EFFECTS ARE STRUCTURAL, NOT A CONVERGENCE FAILURE, BUT ARE
     COUNTED THE SAME WAY. Every method's step-1 final-epoch relative
     improvement is +21.7% to +22.5% (Section 1) simply because step 1 has
     the largest single training-CE drop of the whole run (cold start).
     The blunt "24/40 still improving" headline number includes these 8
     step-1 rows; excluding them would give 16/32 (50%) for steps 2-5
     only -- still a majority, but the raw 60% figure slightly overstates
     how many steps are genuinely under-trained in the CL sense that
     matters (steps 2-5, where forgetting/transfer happen).

  6. NO GENUINE PER-CL-STEP ACCURACY TRAJECTORY (Section 5). Neither run
     retains real 5-point forgetting/backward-transfer curves; the NEW
     run's added schema columns for this are unpopulated. Any forgetting-
     curve claims in the thesis need either a re-run that actually
     populates these fields, or need to be scoped down to what IS
     available (first_step vs later_steps vs all_seen, i.e. old_new_gap).

  7. OVERFITTING IS REAL BUT MILD, NOT YET A CRISIS. The quadrupled
     overfitting rate (Section 3) is a real trend worth acting on before
     pushing epochs further (e.g. to the 8-9 recommended in Section 1),
     but the max single-epoch val-CE regressions are all small (<0.035),
     so this is not (yet) a "the model is memorizing the val-adjacent
     split" level of concern -- more a "the current mitigations
     (dropout=0.1) are under-strength for the epoch budget, scale them
     together" signal.

  8. "LIGHT" EXPORTS LIMIT WHAT CAN BE VERIFIED. Several findings above
     (best-epoch selection not applied, no per-step accuracy) are
     inferred from column-level evidence in the light CSV export, not
     from the full training logs/checkpoints. They should be confirmed
     against the actual training code/full logs before being stated as
     fact in a defense.

RECOMMENDED SETTINGS FOR THE NEXT RUN
==============================================================================

  1. EPOCHS: raise to 8-9/step for a flat budget, or split by family:
     KD methods (T=2) can likely stop at 6-7; non-KD methods (SimpleAvg,
     RankExt, and their FactorOrth variants without KD) need ~9-10 to
     cross this run's own 3% convergence bar (Section 1).
  2. Verify (in code, not just logs) that best-epoch checkpoint selection
     is actually wired into the weights that get merged/evaluated per CL
     step -- current evidence suggests it is not (Section 3).
  3. If EPOCHS goes to 8-9, raise regularization further (dropout beyond
     0.1 and/or add or increase weight decay) given the overfitting-rate
     trend already observed going 3->6 epochs (Section 3).
  4. Actually populate per-step (1-5) accuracy / backward_transfer /
     forward_transfer -- the columns exist in the schema now, just wire up
     the data (Section 5).
  5. Run at least 2-3 additional seeds before treating any of the
     rankings, deltas, or "converged" verdicts in this report as final for
     a thesis defense (Section 6, weakness #1).
