Q1 -- why factor-orth damages old-class (first_step) performance
--------------------------------------------------------------------------

We now have proof, not just hypothesis. I added per-module logging of each
task's LoRA delta norm and its cosine similarity to task 1's delta, both
before and after the final merge, for all 4 simple-average variants. The
result is more interesting than the story we assumed: factor-orth does push
cos(dW1, dWt) slightly closer to zero for tasks 2-5 (0.002 vs 0.012 for plain
averaging), but plain averaging is ALREADY close to orthogonal on its own --
independently-trained adapters in a space this large end up near-orthogonal
by chance, with or without a penalty -- so that's a small effect. And
critically, the merged delta's DIRECTION is actually MORE aligned with task
1's own direction under factor-orth (cosine 0.60) than under plain averaging
(cosine 0.46) -- the opposite of "orthogonal averaging cancels step 1." What
actually drives the damage is magnitude, not direction: factor-orth
measurably shrinks how large tasks 2-5's own updates can grow (their norm
relative to task 1's drops from ~0.91x to ~0.70x), and because we merge by
simple, equal-weighted averaging regardless of each task's update size, task
1's own contribution ends up representing a smaller slice of a smaller total.
Concretely: only 34% of task 1's own trained-delta magnitude survives into
the final merged weight under factor-orth, versus 46% under plain averaging
and 66% under KD alone -- and that retained-magnitude percentage puts all 4
variants in exactly the same rank order as their first_step accuracy (82%,
88.9%, 77.8%, 71.2% -- correlation 0.95). This is why rank_extension never
shows this damage: it doesn't merge anything. Old rank blocks are frozen and
concatenated, confirmed bit-for-bit unchanged (max diff = 0.0) at every step
in this run too, so there's no "merged weight" for this dilution to act on at
all. Bottom line: the damage is real, it's specific to simple_avg's averaging
step, and it's a magnitude-suppression effect from the orthogonality penalty
on later tasks, not a directional-cancellation effect as originally
suspected -- the classifier calibration and wider target-module fix already
in the pipeline cut the worst of it (from a near-total 54%->0.8% wipeout down
to a graded 82%->71% trade-off, where steps 2-5 actually improve in exchange).

Q2 -- what's "combined_..." and why did the blue curve bend
--------------------------------------------------------------------------

This was already resolved last round and is now re-verified on the new
instrumented run's data: "blue" is the Base variant (no KD, no orth), and the
apparent "bend" is a rendering artifact of putting Base's total loss on a
shared log axis alongside variants whose factor-orth term spikes 100-3000x
higher at the start of each step -- not a bug in the numbers. On this run, I
re-confirmed the identity that makes this provable: for Base, train CE and
train TOTAL loss are IDENTICAL to floating-point precision (max difference =
0.0, all 90 rows, both families, every step and epoch) because KD and
factor-orth are exactly zero for that variant. The figure has been
permanently redesigned (one panel per variant, all four loss components
plus TOTAL sharing one log axis with a proper legend) so "TOTAL = sum of the
parts" is checkable by eye within a single panel -- regenerated here from
this run's saved CSVs and it renders and verifies correctly end to end.

Q3 -- why rank_ext's KD curve rises before it decays
--------------------------------------------------------------------------

Confirmed again in this run, and we now also know what the orth-lambda
warmup does to it. In the control (plain KD, no orth loss), KD still rises
26-35% from epoch 1 to epoch 2 at every step transition, landing in the exact
same epoch where CE drops fastest (73-78%) -- unchanged from every prior run,
as expected since this method has no orth loss for the warmup to touch. In
the variant that DOES use the new orth-lambda warmup (factor-orth + KD), the
same rise looks far more dramatic in percentage terms (75% up to 386% across
steps 2-5) -- but that's a start-point artifact, not a different mechanism:
the epoch-2 peak and the epoch-9 steady state are both nearly identical to
the control's at every step. What the warmup actually does is suppress how
much the model drifts in epoch 1 (the still-active, if damped, orthogonality
term competes with CE for gradient budget that epoch), so the model stays
closer to the frozen KD teacher through epoch 1 -- and that epoch-1 floor
keeps getting lower at later steps because the orthogonality constraint
itself gets harder to satisfy as more frozen blocks accumulate. Once the
warmup's influence fades and the new block's own CE-driven adaptation takes
over in epoch 2, KD jumps back up to the same level it would have reached
anyway. In short: the underlying cause (frozen old blocks and the new block
sharing the same weight, so fast new-class fitting necessarily pulls the
combined output away from the frozen teacher) is unchanged and is a
structural property of the architecture, not a bug -- the warmup just delays
when that tug-of-war becomes visible, it doesn't remove it.
