Run under investigation: R3/results_strict_20260717_light/
  clip_vit_lora_cifar100_full_comparison_with_orth_rankext_EPOCH3_MAIN_20260717_015946
Method: recomputation from saved CSVs only (training_loss_history_by_epoch.csv,

HEADLINE ANSWER: this is real, mild, bounded overfitting — the classic shape
(train loss keeps falling, val loss bottoms out then drifts slightly back up),
not a train/val distribution mismatch. It is already being actively and
substantially counteracted by best-epoch selection (fires in 38/40 (method,
step) pairs, is not cosmetic). What closes the remaining gap between "val CE
looks mediocre" and "final accuracy is 70-75%" is (a) CE~0.1-0.35 is not
actually a bad number — it corresponds to fairly high average confidence in
the correct class — and (b) for simple_avg specifically, the final merge step
itself acts as an extra, uncounted regularizer: it measurably shrinks each
specialist's contribution by roughly half, which is exactly what quiets down
per-specialist overfitting noise before the model is ever scored. Nothing
here indicates a bug. Two small, non-urgent improvements are suggested for
the next run (Section 6).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. DOES THE GAP GROW WITHIN EACH STEP (OVERFITTING) OR STAY FLAT (DISTRIBUTION
   MISMATCH)?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
Computed gap = val_ce_loss - train_ce_loss for all 8 methods x 5 steps x 9
epochs (360 rows, training_loss_history_by_epoch.csv). Two regimes, cleanly
separated by epoch:

  EPOCH 1: gap is strongly NEGATIVE for every single (method, step) — e.g.
  simple_avg step 1: -0.921; rank_extension_orth_factor_lam_50 step 5: -5.369.
  This is a labeling/measurement-timing artifact, not a real "val beats
  train" phenomenon — confirmed directly from the raw batch log
  (logs/training_loss_history_by_batch.csv, simple_avg/step=1/epoch<1.0, 594
  batches): the FIRST batch of epoch 1 has ce_loss=4.79 (a freshly-initialized
  classifier, near ln(100)=4.6 chance-level loss over 100 classes), the LAST
  5 batches have ce_loss in [0.05, 0.50], and the epoch's MEAN over all 594
  batches (what gets logged as that epoch's "train_ce_loss") is 1.198 — while
  val_ce_loss (0.208) is a single END-OF-EPOCH snapshot, much closer to the
  epoch's last few batches than to its noisy early-batch average. train_ce_
  loss in this pipeline is an epoch-wide running average (standard HF
  Trainer behavior); val_ce_loss is a point-in-time evaluation. Comparing an
  average-over-training-progress number to a snapshot number always looks
  like "val is better" in epoch 1 specifically, regardless of what's
  actually happening — an artifact of what's being averaged, not of model
  behavior. Not a bug, but worth documenting so a reader doesn't misread
  epoch 1's negative gap as "the model generalizes better than it fits."

  EPOCH 2 ONWARD: gap turns positive and GROWS MONOTONICALLY (with plateauing,
  not runaway divergence) in essentially every (method, step) — e.g.
  rank_extension step 1: epoch2=-0.012 -> epoch4=0.136 -> epoch9=0.203 (rises
  then flattens); simple_avg step 3: epoch2=-0.031 -> epoch5=0.230 ->
  epoch9=0.284 (rises further, does not flatten as cleanly). This is the
  textbook overfitting shape: train CE keeps approaching 0 while val CE
  bottoms out early and either plateaus or drifts back up. Confirmed
  quantitatively for all 40 (method, step) pairs via val_ce_loss's own
  trajectory (not just the gap): every pair's val CE reaches its MINIMUM at
  an epoch strictly before 9 in 38/40 cases (see Section 3), then rises by a
  small but nonzero amount (median +0.021, up to +0.116 for simple_avg step
  3) by epoch 9. Final-epoch (9) train CE across all methods/steps is 0.007-
  0.06 for non-KD methods and 0.24-0.71 for KD methods (still much lower than
  their own val CE of 0.11-0.28 and 0.36-0.93 respectively) — train CE really
  does approach a floor near 0 (near-perfect fit to the ~9,500 training
  images) while val CE settles at a materially higher floor. This is genuine
  memorization of the small per-step training set outpacing generalization,
  not two different data distributions being compared.

2. SAMPLE SIZE CHECK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
tables/validation_split_summary.csv: exactly 475 train / 25 val images for
EVERY one of the 100 classes, std=0 — a uniform, deterministic 95%/5% split
of CIFAR-100's 500 train images per class (not touching the separate 100-
image/class test split used for final accuracy). Per CL step (20 classes):

    train = 20 x 475 = 9,500 images
    val   = 20 x  25 =   500 images   (5% holdout)

This is IDENTICAL for all 8 methods — tables/method_hyperparameter_summary
.csv confirms replay_per_class=0 for every method in this run, so no method
gets extra training data or a different-sized val set. The split is
representative by construction (stratified evenly across every class, drawn
from the same pool as the training images, not a different distribution) —
there is no train/val distribution mismatch to explain the gap. The one
legitimate caveat: 500 images (25/class) is a SMALL val set, which increases
the sampling noise of any single epoch's val_ce_loss estimate relative to
train_ce_loss's much larger 9,500-image base — this explains some of the
epoch-to-epoch JITTER visible in the convergence plots' val curves, but not
the systematic, monotonically-growing GAP documented in Section 1 (sampling
noise would fluctuate, not grow in a consistent direction across 40
independent method/step pairs).

3. BEST-EPOCH SELECTION: IS IT FIRING, AND HOW MUCH DOES IT RECOVER?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
From tables/best_epoch_selected_by_method_step.csv (40 rows = 8 methods x 5
steps): selection fires (selected_epoch < 9) in 38/40 pairs (95%). The 2
exceptions (rank_extension step 5, simple_avg_factor_orth step 4) select
epoch 9 itself — i.e. val CE was still improving (or tied) at the final
epoch for those two, no early stop needed.

recovery = final_epoch_val_ce - selected_val_ce (how much worse the final-
epoch checkpoint would have been vs. the one actually kept):

  Mean recovery, all 40 pairs:         0.0240 CE
  Mean recovery, the 38 that fired:    0.0253 CE
  Mean recovery, rank_extension family: 0.0182 CE
  Mean recovery, simple_avg family:     0.0298 CE
  Largest single recovery: simple_avg step 3 (selected epoch 2 of 9,
    selected_val_ce=0.183 vs. final_epoch_val_ce=0.299 -- 0.116 CE recovered)
  Second largest: simple_avg step 4 (selected epoch 2 of 9, recovery=0.088)

simple_avg selects its best epoch VERY early for 3 of its 5 steps (epoch 2,
2, 2 for steps 2, 3, 4) — far earlier than rank_extension, which typically
selects around epoch 3-8. This lines up with simple_avg's head_lr_multiplier
=10.0 vs. rank_extension's 1.0 (family-conditional setting, unrelated to
this investigation but directly explains the pattern here): a 10x higher
classifier-head learning rate converges — and starts overfitting — much
faster in raw epoch-count, so simple_avg's own best-epoch-selection curve
peaks and turns over sooner. This is not a flaw in the selection mechanism;
it is doing exactly its job (picking the epoch that minimizes held-out CE),
and it is recovering a real, non-trivial amount of val CE specifically where
overfitting is largest (simple_avg's later steps).

4. RECONCILING WITH FINAL ACCURACY: HOW DOES 72-75% ALL_SEEN COME FROM
   VAL CE ~0.1-0.35?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
Two things resolve this, neither of which requires "the val CE numbers are
misleading" — they are consistent, just require translating CE into accuracy
terms and accounting for what the merge step does.

(a) CE ~0.1-0.35 is not actually bad. Cross-entropy on the TRUE class only
    (not the full distribution) corresponds roughly to -log(p_true); CE=0.20
    implies an average assigned probability to the correct class of about
    e^-0.20 ~= 0.82 (~82% average confidence). This lines up closely with
    simple_avg's own recorded first-step accuracy (82.0%, per_step_accuracy_
    by_method.csv) and simple_avg_factor_orth's step-1 val CE (0.193 ->
    implied ~82% confidence) against its actual step-1 accuracy (71.2% —
    same ballpark, allowing for calibration slack and the CE-vs-accuracy gap
    that always exists even in a well-behaved model). A specialist's own
    validation CE in the 0.1-0.35 range is a GOOD score for a 20-way-relevant
    classification problem, not a red flag — "val CE isn't 0" does not mean
    "the model doesn't work," and the actual downstream accuracy is
    consistent with, not contradicted by, these CE values.

(b) The merge step is an uncounted extra regularizer for simple_avg. No
    post-merge validation CE is currently logged anywhere in this pipeline —
    val_total_loss/val CE columns are only ever populated for the individual
    per-step SPECIALIST models (each independently fine-tuned, pre-merge);
    the FINAL merged model is only ever scored on TEST-set accuracy (per_step
    _accuracy_by_method.csv, evaluate_model's 3 aggregate groups), never
    against a held-out val CE. This is a real gap in the current logging
    (see Section 6, recommendation), but the merge_mechanism_by_method_step
    .csv table (added in the prior session's strict review) gives strong
    indirect evidence for what merging does: averaged over all 48 target-
    module x layer combinations, the merged delta's norm is only
    46-53% of the average INDIVIDUAL specialist's own delta norm
    (simple_avg: 0.491, simple_avg_kd_T2: 0.530, simple_avg_factor_orth:
    0.460, simple_avg_factor_orth_kd_T2: 0.461 -- all comfortably below 1.0,
    all close to the classic sqrt(1/5)=0.447 variance-reduction ratio for
    averaging ~5 partially-independent random directions). This means
    merging doesn't just "average 5 overfit specialists into one overfit
    model" — it substantially DAMPS each specialist's individual (partly
    overfit, partly genuinely task-specific) weight change, the same way
    weight-averaging/model-souping techniques in the wider literature are
    known to reduce variance and improve generalization relative to any
    single fine-tuned checkpoint. This is the mechanism that lets a merged
    model outperform what any one specialist's own val CE alone would
    predict, and it is specific to the simple_avg family (rank_extension
    doesn't merge — it grows a single persistent model, so this smoothing
    effect does not apply there, and rank_extension's train-val gaps and
    recoveries are correspondingly smaller and later-onset, consistent with
    Sections 1 and 3).

Net: simple_avg's chain is "each specialist overfits its own 9,500 images
mildly, but (i) even its overfit val CE corresponds to a genuinely good
~80%+ confidence level, (ii) best-epoch selection already pulls back a real
chunk of that overfitting per specialist, and (iii) merging 5 specialists
together further damps whatever overfit-specific noise survives (i) and
(ii)." All 3 stages point the same direction (toward the merged model being
more reliable than any one specialist's raw val CE would suggest), so a
72-75% all_seen accuracy from specialists whose val CE looks like "0.1-0.35"
is expected, not surprising, and not evidence the val CE numbers are wrong.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. IS THIS A PROBLEM?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------
No — by every check performed here, this is ordinary, bounded overfitting of
the kind expected when fine-tuning a large model on ~9,500 images per CL
step for 9 epochs, and it is already actively mitigated:
  - The gap plateaus rather than diverging for most (method, step) pairs
    (Section 1) — the "runaway overfitting" failure mode (val loss climbing
    without bound) is not what's happening here.
  - Best-epoch selection is doing real, substantial work (Section 3), not
    sitting idle — median recovery ~0.02 CE, with the two most-overfitting
    (method, step) pairs recovering 0.09-0.12 CE, is a meaningful correction,
    not noise-level.
  - The magnitude of val CE at its floor (0.1-0.35 for most methods) is
    consistent with the actually-reported accuracy numbers, not in tension
    with them (Section 4a).
  - For simple_avg specifically, the merge step provides an additional,
    measurable damping effect on top of best-epoch selection (Section 4b).

   and a point-in-time val metric, not a real effect. Not fixed this
   session (analysis-only task, no code changes made).
3. simple_avg's very early best-epoch selection (epoch 2 of 9, 3 of its 5
   steps) suggests its head_lr_multiplier=10.0 may be pushing convergence
   (and overfitting onset) faster than the other family-conditional
   settings account for — worth watching if a future run increases EPOCHS
   further, since more headroom for the same 10x LR could widen the
   recoverable gap further; not a change to make now, since best-epoch
   selection is already absorbing it cleanly at the current EPOCHS=9, and
   this multiplier was independently tuned/justified in the prior session's
   audit trail for its effect on final accuracy (not on val-CE overfitting
   specifically, but the two are not in tension based on this investigation).
4. Consider a slightly larger val holdout (e.g. 35-40/class instead of 25)
   if best-epoch selection's noise sensitivity ever becomes a concern —
   current results already look sensible and monotonic enough (Section 1)
   that this is a "nice to have," not something the data here shows is
   actually needed.
